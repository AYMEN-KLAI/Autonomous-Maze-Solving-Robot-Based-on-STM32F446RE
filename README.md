# Autonomous-Maze-Solving-Robot-Based-on-STM32F446RE
##1. Introduction and Project Genesis
This technical report document outlines the engineering, structural architecture, and algorithmic implementation of an advanced autonomous maze-solving mobile robot. Built from the ground up as a high-performance mechatronic system, this prototype was specifically engineered to tackle complex, unknown labyrinthine environments under strict real-time constraints. The main processing unit driving the system is the robust STM32F446RET6 microcontroller, which provides the computational throughput required for concurrent sensor polling, tight motion control loops, and dynamic path-planning execution. By executing an optimized hardware-software co-design, this autonomous platform successfully demonstrated an exceptional balance of speed, structural reliability, and intelligent navigation during live trials. Consequently, this innovative robotic system achieved widespread recognition, proudly securing the 2nd Place award in the highly competitive Maze Runner category at the prestigious Robolimpix 4.0 tournament.
##2. Core Control Unit and Microcontroller Performance
At the heart of the robot's electrical architecture lies the NUCLEO-F446RE development board, powered by an ARM Cortex-M4 core operating at a core clock frequency of 168,MHz. This processing capability is further augmented by a hardware Floating Point Unit (FPU), which plays a critical role in accelerating real-time kinematic calculations and odometry updates without introducing latency. The system configuration leverages the internal clock tree via an external 8,MHz oscillator (HSE), multiplying the frequency through the main Phase-Locked Loop (PLL) to supply the AHB and APB peripheral buses with maximum bandwidth. This specific hardware selection allows the firmware to leverage advanced internal peripherals, such as hardware Quadrature Encoder Interface (QEI) modes and dedicated high-frequency timers, ensuring that raw environmental data is processed instantly to avoid any mechanical stalling during exploration.
##3. Sensor Integration and Environmental Mapping
To safely navigate through a matrix of walls and narrow corridors, the robot relies on a comprehensive array of spatial perception sensors. Frontal and lateral obstacle detection is handled by three precisely positioned HC-SR04 ultrasonic transducer modules, which are mapped to the front, left, and right faces of the chassis. These sensors operate by emitting high-frequency acoustic bursts triggered by short 10,$\mu$s pulses from the microcontroller, while a highly accurate 32-bit hardware timer (TIM5) measures the exact time-of-flight of the incoming Echo signals with a resolution of 1,$\mu$s per tick. Because the Echo outputs operate at a 5,V logical level, passive resistor divider networks (1,k$\Omega$ and 2,k$\Omega$) are integrated into the signal lines to shift the voltage down to a safe 3.3,V level, protecting the input GPIO pins of the STM32. This acoustic telemetry loop allows the robot to compute wall distances continuously, preventing collision risks within a tight 25,ms safety timeout.
##4. Inertial Navigation and Trajectory Correction
In addition to distance ranging, maintaining a straight heading and executing perfect 90-degree rotations requires advanced inertial monitoring. This is achieved by integrating an MPU-6050 Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU), which communicates with the main processor via a Fast-Mode I2C bus operating at 400,kHz. During the initialization phase, a custom calibration routine samples the gyroscope offset across 200 consecutive readings while the chassis is at rest. During runtime, the firmware continuously integrates the angular velocity data from the Z-axis to track the robot's absolute heading in real time. This inertial feedback is combined with a dual-channel hardware encoder setup using Timer 2 and Timer 3 in full quadrature mode, allowing the system to cross-reference physical wheel revolutions with the gyroscope data. This closed-loop sensor fusion algorithm successfully eliminates slip errors caused by uneven friction on the competition tracks.
##5. Actuation and Power Distribution
The physical movement of the decagonal chassis is driven by two high-torque direct-current motors configured as a differential drive steering system. Power delivery is managed by two individual BTS7960B high-current half-bridge motor drivers capable of handling heavy electrical loads. Speed modulation is implemented using Timer 1, configured to output four distinct center-aligned PWM channels with a resolution of 10,000 steps (ARR = 9999). Operating at a switching frequency of 16.8,kHz, the PWM signals control the H-bridge duty cycles smoothly while remaining completely outside the audible range of human hearing, eliminating motor hum. The power management network relies on a 12,V primary battery source, which directly supplies the motor drivers while dual buck and low-dropout (LDO) regulators step down the voltage to stable 5,V and 3.3,V rails to isolate the sensitive digital logic from high-power inductive switching noise.
##6. Algorithmic Navigation and Flood Fill Optimization
The maze-solving intelligence of the firmware relies heavily on a dynamic implementation of the classic Flood Fill algorithm, which is structurally equivalent to a bounded Breadth-First Search (BFS). Upon powering up, the robot initializes a virtual matrix representing the known layout of the maze cells, with manhattan distances pre-calculated relative to the target coordinates. As the robot moves forward, the distance sensors scan the adjacent cells for physical walls; when a new obstacle is detected, the firmware dynamically modifies the internal wall map and immediately triggers an array-wide re-flooding routine. This rapid calculation updates the cell values instantly, ensuring that the path-planning module always directs the robot toward the lowest neighboring value. If a dead end is detected, the algorithm computes an alternative route on the fly, preventing loops and optimizing the path for subsequent high-speed runs.
##7. Tournament Performance and Competitive Recognition
The ultimate validation of this mechatronic design occurred during the intensive trials of the Robolimpix 4.0 grand arena. Competing against dozens of highly optimized academic and independent engineering teams within the intense Maze Runner category, the robot demonstrated outstanding tactical performance. The combination of hardware-level encoder counting, rapid gyroscope trajectory correction, and efficient C-code memory management allowed the vehicle to map out the maze topology with superior reliability compared to microcontrollers using software-based interrupts. The robot's stability during sudden changes in velocity and its ability to recover instantly from tight corners minimized tracking drift. By seamlessly executing its pathfinding routines and discovering the optimal route to the center goals under intense pressure, this project successfully clinched 2nd Place, solidifying its status as a top-tier reference design for autonomous robotic navigation.
